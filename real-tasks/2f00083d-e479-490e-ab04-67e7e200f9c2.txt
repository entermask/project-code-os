Tea at Six, Death at Seven
A Miss Marple Mystery
Set in 1952, Mosswick-on-Wye, Herefordshire

Chapter One: The Hospitality of Mrs. Brent-Howell
The village of Mosswick-on-Wye had not changed appreciably since the Coronation bunting went up and came down again, though everyone agreed the bunting had been rather better quality than in previous years. The high street retained its familiar arrangement of a post office, a chemist's, a public house called The Crossed Keys, and a tea room called Millicent's, which was run not by any Millicent but by a stout man named Arnold Pruett who had inherited the name along with the lease. The church of St. Cuthbert's stood at the east end of the village with its squat Norman tower, and the houses fanned outward from it in the comfortable, haphazard way of English villages that had never been planned by anyone.
It was into this arrangement that Miss Jane Marple arrived on a Thursday in late September, carrying one large carpetbag, one smaller knitting bag, and an expression of mild, pleasant interest that had served her well for many decades in St. Mary Mead and any number of places besides.
She had come at the invitation of her nephew's former school friend, Dr. Horace Pellew, who served as the village's general practitioner and who had written to say that the air in Mosswick was particularly restorative for elderly ladies who had been suffering from autumn bronchitis. Miss Marple had not been suffering from autumn bronchitis at all, but she had found Dr. Pellew's letter charming nonetheless and had decided that a fortnight away from her own garden, which was looking rather dispiriting after a wet summer, might do her very well.
Dr. Pellew had arranged for her to lodge with Mrs. Dorothy Brent-Howell, a widow of sixty-one who kept a large house called Wisteria Lodge at the northern edge of the village and who supplemented her income by taking in genteel paying guests. Mrs. Brent-Howell had the kind of face that had once been handsome and was now merely formidable, with very bright grey eyes that catalogued whatever they rested upon with the thoroughness of an auctioneer.
"You'll find us quiet," Mrs. Brent-Howell said, when Miss Marple arrived and was installed in a room with a view of the back garden and a writing table with a slightly uneven leg. "We had some excitement in the spring when young Carver's boy drove his bicycle into the war memorial, but that was spring. September is generally very quiet."
"How lovely," said Miss Marple, and meant it entirely.
The paying guests at Wisteria Lodge consisted, in that particular fortnight, of Miss Marple herself and a retired solicitor from Wolverhampton named Mr. Oswald Finch, who was seventy-three, almost completely deaf in his left ear, and profoundly interested in the local church records. He and Miss Marple exchanged the polite, evaluating nods of two people who expected to occupy the same breakfast table for some time and intended to make the best of it.
On the following Sunday, Mrs. Brent-Howell announced at breakfast that she would be hosting her customary tea on the coming Thursday at six o'clock.
"I do it four times a year," she explained, pouring tea with the precision of someone who regarded imprecise pouring as a moral failing. "The village comes. It is, I flatter myself, something of an institution." She paused. "You are both welcome to attend, naturally."
Mr. Finch cupped his better ear and asked what had been said. Miss Marple explained, gently, and he replied that he was very fond of a good tea.
The tea was, in Mosswick's understanding, a significant social event. Miss Marple gathered this from several sources over the following days: from Mrs. Anstey at the post office, who mentioned it while weighing a parcel; from the young woman at Millicent's who brought her a pot of Earl Grey and volunteered that the Brent-Howell teas were always "a real occasion"; and from Dr. Pellew himself, who stopped by on Tuesday to ask after Miss Marple's nonexistent bronchitis and mentioned that he always attended.
"Dorothy sets a magnificent table," he said. He was a lean, sandy-haired man of fifty with kind eyes behind thick spectacles. "Cucumber sandwiches, seed cake, that particular lemon sponge she does. And one meets everyone who is anyone in Mosswick, which is saying something, because Mosswick's population runs to about four hundred and fifty souls and anyone who is anyone is generally there."
"Who is anyone?" Miss Marple asked, with the innocent curiosity she deployed with such precision.
Dr. Pellew counted on his fingers. "The Carver family — George Carver, he farms most of the land east of the church, and his wife Nora. Their daughter Patricia. The Reverend Thurlow and his wife. Ruth Addison, who runs the lending library from her front room. Bernard Croft, who owns The Crossed Keys. And this year there will be the new people — the Whitfields. They've taken Amber Cottage for six months. Hugo Whitfield. He's a painter of some kind. Comes with his wife and his wife's companion, or secretary, or some such arrangement. They arrived in August and have been rather a subject of discussion."
"Discussion?" Miss Marple repeated.
"Well." Dr. Pellew removed his spectacles and polished them, which Miss Marple had noticed was what he did when choosing his words. "Hugo Whitfield is — not universally liked. He is charming, certainly. Very charming. But there is something about him that George Carver, for one, finds rather objectionable. And Nora Carver finds rather the opposite."
He replaced his spectacles and changed the subject with the abruptness of a man who felt he had said more than intended.
Miss Marple knitted and made a mental note.
Thursday arrived with appropriate English weather: a pale sun attempting the morning, defeated by cloud by midday, and a determined fine drizzle beginning at half past four that had resolved into something more committed by six. The guests arrived at Wisteria Lodge with damp umbrellas and the slightly heightened sociability that English people tend to adopt when weather has made them uncomfortable together.
Miss Marple positioned herself near the fireplace in the sitting room — the fire had been lit, sensibly, and the room was warm and smelled of wood smoke and the excellent lemon sponge — with her knitting and her cup of tea and an expression of mild, benign vagueness that she had perfected over many years. From this position she could observe everything and be underestimated by everyone, which was precisely the arrangement she preferred.
The guests were as Dr. Pellew had forecast, and she observed them all.
George Carver was a broad, red-faced man of fifty-five who moved through the room as if it were a field he owned, which several fields in the near vicinity actually were. He spoke in short sentences, laughed loudly at his own remarks, and kept his eyes on his wife with the specific quality of attention that is less affectionate than suspicious.
Nora Carver was ten years younger than her husband and had the slightly pressed look of a woman whose energies have been redirected from their natural channels into channels that don't quite suit them. She was still very pretty in an uncomfortable way, as though prettiness had become inconvenient to her. She spoke to everyone in the room except Hugo Whitfield, which Miss Marple noted with the same careful interest she gave the single exception.
Hugo Whitfield was forty, possibly forty-two, with the kind of dark good looks that had begun to acquire a certain heaviness around the jaw that would, in ten years, make him merely prosperous-looking rather than striking. He talked fluently and with great animation. He was the sort of man who addressed every woman as though she were the most interesting person in the room, and every man as though he were slightly less interesting than Hugo Whitfield. Miss Marple received the full force of his charm when he was introduced to her by Mrs. Brent-Howell and found it considerable and somewhat exhausting.
With him came his wife, Cecily Whitfield, a quiet woman of thirty-eight with reddish-brown hair worn neatly and an air of watchful composure. She was not beautiful, but she had a quality of stillness that drew the eye once one looked away from her husband. She drank her tea and spoke pleasantly when spoken to and watched her husband with an expression Miss Marple could not immediately classify.
The companion — introduced as Miss Vera Dodd — was thirty-four, thin, with pale colouring and clever eyes behind round spectacles. She had the particular quality of invisibility that some women develop when they have spent years in proximity to a more socially dominant personality. She sat near Cecily Whitfield and very rarely looked at Hugo.
The Reverend Thurlow was mild and moon-faced, easily diverted by questions about St. Cuthbert's Norman tower. His wife, Barbara, was the kind of vicar's wife who had decided that energy was a virtue and applied it to every available cause. She was currently applying it to the question of the village hall curtains, which required replacing, and was attempting to form a committee from whatever material was available.
Ruth Addison, who ran the lending library from her front room on Church Lane, was sixty-eight and had the quality of someone who has read a very large number of books and is not entirely surprised by anything human beings choose to do. She settled near Miss Marple with the recognition of two women of similar formation, and they had a comfortable conversation about the inadequacy of recent detective fiction and the superiority of character over plot.
"Character always gives it away," Miss Marple said. "In fiction and in life."
"My view entirely," said Ruth Addison.
Bernard Croft from The Crossed Keys arrived late, with the slightly preoccupied manner of a man who has left someone else in charge of his premises and is not entirely confident in the arrangement. He was forty-seven, stocky, with a publican's sociable face that had learned to be interested in everyone's business while giving nothing away about his own.
The tea proceeded pleasantly. Mrs. Brent-Howell's lemon sponge was indeed excellent. The conversation turned on the usual subjects: the weather, the village hall, a dispute about the placing of a new footpath sign, the general question of whether the bus service to Hereford would be improved or worsened by the coming changes to the county route.
At seven o'clock, Hugo Whitfield excused himself — he had a telephone call expected, he said, smiling generally at the room. He left through the hallway toward the small sitting room Mrs. Brent-Howell made available to her guests, where the telephone was housed.
At seven minutes past seven, a sound reached the sitting room that was not, in itself, remarkable: a single heavy thud from somewhere in the interior of the house, followed by a shorter, sharper sound that might have been anything.
No one did anything for a moment. Then Mrs. Brent-Howell said, "How very odd," and went into the hallway.
Her scream brought everyone out of the sitting room at once.
Hugo Whitfield was at the foot of the back staircase, which rose from a passage connecting the hallway to the kitchen. He was lying face down on the flagstone floor at the bottom of the stairs in a posture that admitted no comfortable interpretation. Dr. Pellew was through the sitting room door and kneeling beside him before anyone else had fully registered what they were seeing.
He looked up after a moment, and his face said what he did not immediately say aloud.
"I'm afraid," he said, carefully, "that Mr. Whitfield is dead."

Chapter Two: The Shape of a Fall
The ambulance from Hereford took forty-three minutes to arrive, during which time the house underwent a peculiar change of atmosphere. The sitting room, which had been warm and social, became merely a room where people sat because there was nowhere else to go. Mrs. Brent-Howell's excellent lemon sponge remained on the table looking irrelevant.
Dr. Pellew had established, with the careful authority of a man experienced in communicating medical facts to distressed people, that Hugo Whitfield had died of a head injury consistent with a fall down a staircase. The back stairs of Wisteria Lodge were steep, uncarpeted at the lower three steps where the covering had worn through and not yet been replaced, and turned at an awkward angle at the bottom. It was, Dr. Pellew indicated, the sort of accident that was entirely plausible.
Constable Hewett of the Mosswick police — a single-officer station, supplemented in serious matters by the larger establishment in Hereford — arrived in eleven minutes and established the basic facts with thoroughness if not great sophistication. He took names, confirmed that no one had been in the passage or near the staircase at the relevant time, and arranged for the body to remain undisturbed until the Hereford constabulary could send an inspector.
Inspector Harbridge arrived with the ambulance and was a compact, practical man who surveyed the scene with the efficiency of someone who has resolved many such situations and expects to resolve this one also. He spoke to Dr. Pellew, examined the staircase, made notes, and then addressed the assembled guests with courtesy and brevity.
"A very unfortunate accident," he said. "The staircase presents an obvious hazard. We'll need statements from everyone, naturally. Mrs. Whitfield, I'm particularly sorry to have to speak with you at such a moment."
Cecily Whitfield had not wept. She sat in the sitting room with her hands folded in her lap, her composure so complete that it had become its own kind of spectacle. Miss Vera Dodd sat beside her with a hand resting very lightly on Cecily's forearm, and Cecily had not moved away from it.
"Of course," Cecily said. "Whatever is required."
Miss Marple, who had remained in her chair throughout, her knitting needles moving with their habitual soft rhythm, watched Cecily Whitfield with the careful attention she generally kept well concealed beneath the mild expression.
It was not grief, she thought. It was not shock, either, at least not the shock of the wholly unexpected. It was something more complex: a person receiving news they had in some way prepared for without knowing exactly when it would arrive.
This was, Miss Marple reflected, a very interesting quality of composure indeed.
George Carver, in the chair beside the window, was doing something different with his composure. He was gripping the arm of the chair with one hand with the tightness of a man suppressing considerable feeling. He was not looking at Nora. Nora, across the room, had gone rather white and was talking in a quick, quiet voice to Ruth Addison, who was listening with the expression of someone receiving information they would catalogue carefully.
Mr. Finch, Miss Marple's fellow lodger, had understood approximately half of the evening's events due to his deafness and was being periodically updated by the Reverend Thurlow, who leaned sideways and spoke carefully into his better ear. Mr. Finch's responses suggested he was finding the situation rather more exciting than distressing.
Bernard Croft from The Crossed Keys was standing near the window with a glass of brandy that Mrs. Brent-Howell had located for the shock and distributed with her usual purposeful efficiency. He was watching Inspector Harbridge with the specific quality of attention that is not curiosity but rather assessment.
Mrs. Brent-Howell herself moved through the room with brandy and tea and small practical suggestions, her grey eyes brighter than usual. She was a woman who, confronted with disorder, responded with hospitality. It was, Miss Marple thought, rather admirable.
Inspector Harbridge took his statements with efficiency. Miss Marple, when her turn came, confirmed her position in the sitting room near the fireplace, her inability to see either the hallway or the passage from that position, and the approximate timing of the thud. She said nothing else, and Harbridge thanked her and moved on.
By nine o'clock, the ambulance had departed with Hugo Whitfield's body, the inspector had collected his statements and departed with the assurance that a full report would follow, and the guests had dispersed into the September drizzle with the subdued air of people who will spend the remainder of the evening discussing nothing else.
Cecily Whitfield and Miss Vera Dodd were driven back to Amber Cottage by Dr. Pellew, who had offered with the quiet competence that characterized him.
Mrs. Brent-Howell, returning from seeing the last guest out, found Miss Marple still in the sitting room with her knitting.
"Good heavens, Miss Marple," she said, not unkindly. "I'd forgotten you were there."
"People rather do," said Miss Marple pleasantly. "Mrs. Brent-Howell, might I ask you one small thing? The back staircase — has it always been uncarpeted on those lower steps?"
Mrs. Brent-Howell looked briefly puzzled. "No, in fact. I'd intended to have it replaced. The carpet came away about a fortnight ago. I'd been meaning to speak to someone about it before now."
"And had Mr. Whitfield ever used that staircase before this evening? In the ordinary course of your gatherings?"
"No reason he should have. The guest sitting room with the telephone is off the main hallway. The back staircase leads to the kitchen and the utility room." She paused. "Why would he have gone that way at all?"
"Why indeed," said Miss Marple, and wound a new length of blue wool around her needle.

Chapter Three: What Mosswick Said Over Breakfast
The following morning, Mosswick said a great deal.
Miss Marple breakfasted early and walked to Millicent's at nine, where the tea room was performing its usual morning function as the village's primary information exchange. She took the small table by the window, ordered tea and a buttered scone, and listened with the patient attention she gave to church bells, which was to say without appearing to listen at all.
Arnold Pruett, moving between tables with the solicitousness of a man who regarded himself as a social institution rather than a tradesman, was in excellent form. He had already spoken to Mrs. Anstey at the post office, who had been at the tea, and he was conveying her account to a young woman named Carol from the village shop with the improvements that come from a second telling.
"Drank too much, is what she thinks," Arnold said, not quite quietly. "These London people. Never can hold it."
"Was he from London?" Carol asked.
"Birmingham, I heard. But same thing, isn't it."
This was not, Miss Marple reflected, particularly illuminating, but it established the village's baseline interpretation: accident, and probably a deserved one.
Dr. Pellew found her there at half past nine and joined her, ordering coffee and looking as though he had not slept entirely well.
"Harbridge is calling it accidental," he said, when Arnold had retreated.
"Do you agree?" Miss Marple asked.
He was silent for a moment. "The physical evidence is consistent with a fall. The lower steps without carpet, the awkward turn at the bottom, a man who had perhaps had two glasses of wine — it's plausible."
"But?"
"But Hugo Whitfield did not drink wine last night. He had asked Dorothy — Mrs. Brent-Howell — for water. He told me in passing that he had some kind of gastric complaint and had been off alcohol for a fortnight." He paused. "Which means the plausibility of the accident rests on certain assumptions about the state of a man who was actually perfectly sober."
"And a sober man on an unfamiliar staircase," Miss Marple said, "would presumably be reasonably careful on uncarpeted steps."
"Reasonably, yes."
"Was the light good in that passage?"
Dr. Pellew thought. "There's a single bulb. I noticed it when I went through to examine him. It was working."
"The passage is not entirely dark at the far end, either," Miss Marple said. "There is a window above the utility room door. Even at seven in the evening in late September, there would have been some residual light."
Pellew looked at her with the expression of a man revising his assessment of a fellow diner. "You've been thinking about this."
"I have been thinking," Miss Marple agreed, "about the question of why Mr. Whitfield went down that passage at all. The guest sitting room with the telephone is off the main hall, not past the back staircase. For him to have reached that staircase, he would have had to pass through the door at the end of the hallway and into the passage that connects to the kitchen."
"Which he had no particular reason to do."
"None that we know of," said Miss Marple. "Unless he was going to meet someone. Or following someone. Or was directed there."
Pellew put down his coffee cup. "Directed."
"It would be very easy, at a tea party, to pass someone a note. Or to whisper that you'd like a word in private and suggest somewhere quiet. The back passage is quiet. Rather too quiet, as it happened."
There was a pause.
"I ought to ring Harbridge," Dr. Pellew said slowly.
"It might be worth raising the question," Miss Marple said, with the equanimity of a woman who expected the question to be raised and had decided not to do the raising herself just yet. "Tell me — you mentioned that Hugo Whitfield was not universally liked. George Carver found him objectionable, you said."
"Yes." Pellew's voice was careful again. "George Carver is a straightforward man, in the sense that he shows what he feels, which is not the same as being a simple man. He felt that Whitfield had — paid too much attention to Nora."
"And had he? Paid too much attention?"
"I think," Pellew said, "that is a question rather above my diagnostic capacity."
Miss Marple smiled. "And Miss Vera Dodd? The companion?"
"I know very little about her. She came with the Whitfields in August. Quiet woman. I treated her for a sprained wrist in her first week here — she said she'd caught it on a garden gate."
"Had she?"
Pellew looked at her again with that quality of reassessment. "I assumed so. She seemed — not distressed, exactly. Careful. As though she were choosing her words."
"Yes," said Miss Marple. "She struck me that way also." She lifted her teacup. "I think I will call on Mrs. Whitfield this afternoon. To offer condolences. It is what one does."

Chapter Four: Amber Cottage in the Afternoon
Amber Cottage was a solid stone building set back from the lane that ran westward from the church, with a garden that the Whitfields had not had time or inclination to improve from its August state of mild overgrowth. A japonica sprawled across the front wall with the confidence of a plant that had decided the house was its property, and the roses needed cutting back.
Miss Marple arrived at three o'clock with a pot of honey from Mrs. Brent-Howell's kitchen — pressed upon her by that lady, who felt that honey was an appropriate sympathy offering — and knocked at the door.
It was Vera Dodd who answered. She wore the same round spectacles and the same quality of careful self-containment as the previous evening, but she looked as though she had slept less than Dr. Pellew.
"Miss Marple," she said, without visible surprise.
"I do hope I am not intruding," Miss Marple said, with the mild concern of someone who hoped nothing of the kind. "I simply wished to offer Mrs. Whitfield my condolences."
Vera Dodd held the door open.
The sitting room of Amber Cottage was a comfortable disorder of books, painting materials, and the portable domestic arrangements of people who have established themselves temporarily somewhere. Hugo Whitfield's presence was everywhere and very suddenly absent: a half-finished canvas on an easel near the window, a pair of reading glasses on the arm of a chair, a man's jacket on the coat stand.
Cecily Whitfield was on the sofa with her feet tucked under her and a cup of tea she had not been drinking going cold on the table before her. She looked up when Miss Marple came in and her expression was not hostile, which was something.
"How kind of you," she said, and meant it in a way that was perhaps not precisely the way Miss Marple had intended.
Miss Marple sat in the offered chair and accepted a cup of tea from Vera, who brought one without being asked, and they talked of small things for ten minutes with the politeness that grief sometimes requires before the real conversation can begin.
It was Cecily who shifted the subject.
"You were sitting near the fireplace all evening," she said. "I noticed you. You notice things, don't you? I could see it."
"I am a very old woman at a tea party," Miss Marple said. "There is not much else to do but observe."
Cecily almost smiled. "Do you think it was an accident?"
The question sat in the room with some weight.
"I think," Miss Marple said carefully, "that the inspector believes it was."
"That is not what I asked."
Miss Marple looked at her hands. "No. I suppose it isn't." A pause. "Mrs. Whitfield, your husband went toward the back of the house. Is there anything — any reason he might have done that?"
Cecily was quiet for a moment. Vera, in the chair across the room, was quite still.
"He might have been meeting someone," Cecily said. "He was that kind of man. He arranged private meetings. He enjoyed — the discrete management of his social life." Her voice was flat, without bitterness, which was in its way worse. "There was always someone he was managing."
"Someone at the tea?"
"Quite possibly." She paused. "Miss Marple, I am going to tell you something that I would not tell the inspector, because the inspector would turn it into something it is not and I have had enough disorder. My husband and I had been living separately, in practical terms, for rather more than a year. We maintained the appearance of a marriage because it was convenient to both of us. Hugo required a wife in the social sense. I required — time. Time to determine what I would do next."
"And Miss Dodd?" Miss Marple asked gently.
The silence was brief but sufficient.
"Vera has been my very great friend for three years," Cecily said. "She came with us to Mosswick because I asked her to come. Hugo had no objection. He was, in most practical matters, not an unkind man."
Miss Marple looked at Vera Dodd, who was looking at Cecily, and the quality of that gaze was something Miss Marple filed away with care.
"Mrs. Whitfield," she said, "did your husband have enemies? People who might have wished him serious harm?"
Cecily considered this with the thoroughness of a woman who does not answer important questions hastily.
"Hugo moved through people," she said at last. "He used what was useful and set aside what was not. Over twenty years, that kind of management accumulates a certain amount of wreckage. Whether anyone from the wreckage followed him to Mosswick—" She stopped. "There is George Carver."
"Yes."
"Hugo and Nora Carver had been — friendly. Before we came here, in fact. I don't know the full extent of it. I didn't ask. But George Carver knew, or suspected, and men like George Carver carry things like that in a particular way."
"A particular way," Miss Marple repeated.
"Like a stone in a closed fist," Cecily said. "For a very long time. And then one day the fist opens."
Miss Marple thanked her for the tea and the conversation, pressed the honey upon Vera Dodd with gentle insistence, and walked back through the lane to Wisteria Lodge in the mild September afternoon with her carpetbag on her arm and a great deal to think about.

Chapter Five: The Staircase and the Carpet
On Saturday morning, while Mr. Finch was at St. Cuthbert's examining the parish registers and Mrs. Brent-Howell was engaged with her weekly arrangement of the house, Miss Marple asked if she might have another look at the back staircase.
Mrs. Brent-Howell, who by now had the intuition that Miss Marple's mild requests tended to have particular purposes, showed her through the hallway door and into the passage without comment.
The passage was narrow and smelled of old stone and cleaning product. The back staircase rose at the far end, steep and with the characteristic right-angle turn three steps from the bottom. The single overhead bulb was adequate. The floor at the base of the stairs was the same flagstone as the rest of the passage, and it was here that Hugo Whitfield had been found.
Miss Marple looked at the lower three steps, which were indeed uncarpeted, the raw wood edges showing where the covering had been removed. She looked at the wall on the left side of the staircase, which had no banister — a banister ran on the right side only, fixed to the wall with two iron brackets. She looked at the flagstones.
"Mrs. Brent-Howell," she said, "where does the carpet from these steps currently reside?"
"I've put it in the utility room. I haven't disposed of it yet — been meaning to find someone to quote me for a replacement."
The utility room was through a door at the far end of the passage. The roll of old stair carpet — not a full roll, merely three short sections — was propped against the wall behind the hot water cylinder. Miss Marple examined the underside of the lowest section with the methodical attention she gave to seedlings.
"The fixings have been removed," she said. "The tacks."
"Yes, that's right. I had Mrs. Hedges' husband do it. He was in doing some other jobs and I asked him to take up the carpet at the same time."
"When was this, exactly?"
"Let me think." Mrs. Brent-Howell frowned. "The Saturday before last. Nearly a fortnight ago."
"And the carpet has been in here since."
"Yes."
Miss Marple straightened up and looked at the section of carpet with an expression that someone watching closely might have described as satisfied.
"The tack holes," she said, "are in the expected places for the sides of the steps. But there are additional marks here, near the center of the lower section. As though something had been attached more recently and then removed."
Mrs. Brent-Howell looked where Miss Marple indicated. The marks were small — two of them, three inches apart, in the center of the carpet strip that would have laid across the lowest of the uncovered steps.
"I don't know what those are from," Mrs. Brent-Howell said slowly. "They weren't there when it came up."
"No," said Miss Marple. "I don't suppose they were."
She did not explain further, which was perhaps why Mrs. Brent-Howell later described her, in conversation with Dr. Pellew, as "that remarkable woman who says half of what she means and somehow implies the rest."
What Miss Marple was thinking about, as she returned to the sitting room and her knitting, was a piece of wire or thin cord, fixed across the lowest step of the uncarpeted staircase at ankle height, designed to catch the foot of someone descending in the reasonable confidence that it would look, afterward, like a fall.

Chapter Six: The Lending Library and Ruth Addison
Ruth Addison's lending library occupied the front room of 4 Church Lane, which had been a lending library since Ruth was thirty-two and had remained one because Ruth, now sixty-eight, had not changed her mind about it. The room held approximately eight hundred volumes in shelves that had been purpose-built by Ruth's late husband, a man who believed that shelving was the foundation of civilization, and a small desk where Ruth sat with a ledger and her card index and her full and accurate knowledge of who had borrowed what and when and whether they had returned it.
Miss Marple arrived on Saturday afternoon and browsed for a few minutes with the genuine pleasure of someone who likes books, then selected a volume of essays on the English garden and brought it to the desk.
"Dr. Pellew mentioned your library," she said.
"Everyone ends up here eventually," Ruth Addison said, stamping the card with the comfortable authority of a woman at the center of her own domain. "Even people who don't read much. It's a quiet place."
"I imagine you hear a great deal, in a quiet place."
Ruth Addison looked at her over the rims of her reading glasses with the expression of someone who has been having this conversation in various forms for thirty-six years. "I imagine you do too, Miss Marple."
Miss Marple smiled. "The Whitfields. Did they use the library?"
"She did. Cecily. She came in three times. Borrowed Trollope the first visit, which tells you something. Vera Dodd borrowed detective stories, which may or may not tell you something. Hugo Whitfield came in once, browsed, and left without borrowing anything."
"Did he speak to you?"
"He asked if I had anything on local history. I suggested the Mosswick parish history, which is a perfectly respectable volume published in 1934 by the Reverend Glover. He looked at it for about five minutes, then asked me if the Carver family had farmed here for long."
Miss Marple was quiet for a moment, winding blue wool.
"The Carver family," she said.
"Four generations. George Carver's grandfather broke the land east of the church. It's good farming country." Ruth paused. "Hugo Whitfield seemed interested in that. He asked about the current arrangement — who owned the farm, who inherited. I told him it was George and that George had one daughter, Patricia, who would presumably have it after him."
"And Nora Carver? Did he ask about Nora?"
Ruth Addison set down her stamp with deliberate care. "He asked if Nora Carver had been a local girl. I said no, she'd come from Ledbury and married George seventeen years ago."
"Did that seem to interest him?"
"It seemed to confirm something for him, I would say. He had the look of a man whose arithmetic has come out correctly." Ruth paused. "What are you looking for, Miss Marple?"
"I am trying to understand," Miss Marple said, "what brought the Whitfields to Mosswick. Because people come to villages for reasons: for quiet, for beauty, for health, for cheap rent. But sometimes they come because someone they know is already there. And when that is the case, the question of which someone and what precisely is their connection to the arriving party becomes, as my nephew used to say, rather germane."
Ruth Addison was quiet for a moment.
"Patricia Carver came in on Wednesday morning," she said. "The day after Hugo Whitfield died. She borrowed three novels and looked as though she hadn't slept. She's twenty-two. She has her mother's coloring and her father's way of holding herself — very upright, as though she's been told that posture is character."
"Did she say anything?"
"She said — and I thought it odd at the time — she said, 'I'm glad books don't talk back.' Then she seemed to hear herself and went red and left rather quickly."
Miss Marple looked at her essay collection and then at Ruth Addison.
"I don't suppose," she said, "that you know where Patricia Carver is likely to be on a Saturday afternoon?"
"The farm, usually. Her father keeps her busy."
"And her mother?"
Ruth Addison's expression was precise and careful. "Nora Carver takes a walk along the river path most Saturday afternoons. She has done since I've known her, which is ten years. She walks as far as the old mill and comes back. Alone."
"Thank you," said Miss Marple. "The essays look very good."
"They are. Come back when you've finished them."

Chapter Seven: By the River
The path to the old mill ran along the west bank of the Wye, descending from the village through a meadow that was still green in September and then following the river for half a mile through alders and willows to the ruin of a Victorian mill that had worked until 1921 and had been picturesque ever since.
Miss Marple was no great walker, but she was a determined one, and she had equipped herself with a stout stick borrowed from Mrs. Brent-Howell's hallway stand and a good pair of shoes. She reached the meadow at half past three and saw Nora Carver ahead of her on the path, already some distance from the village.
She did not hurry. She walked at her own pace and let the river do its work, which was to provide a neutral subject for conversation when conversation became necessary.
Nora Carver heard her coming on the path and turned, and her expression cycled through surprise, calculation, and finally something that settled into cautious cordiality.
"Miss Marple," she said. "You're a walker?"
"In a modest way." Miss Marple reached her level and stopped, looking out at the river with the genuine appreciation of someone who likes water. "What a fine afternoon."
They walked together without plan, which was to say by Nora's silent assent and Miss Marple's gentle assumption. For the first ten minutes they talked about the river, the mill, the history of the Wye valley, and whether the autumn had been wetter than usual. Nora Carver was an intelligent woman and she walked with the specific quality of someone for whom walking was a private necessity rather than a leisure activity, and she was perhaps ten years overdue for a conversation that she had not been able to have with anyone.
"You spoke to Cecily Whitfield," she said, when the mill came into view. It was not a question.
"I called to offer condolences," Miss Marple said.
"Yes." A pause. "I couldn't. I wanted to. It seemed — impossible."
"Because of your acquaintance with her husband."
Nora stopped walking. They were at the mill now, standing on the old stone platform where the wheel had once been. The river moved below them with quiet persistence.
"I was a fool," Nora said, with the flatness of someone stating a fact that has been examined from every angle and remains unchanged. "Three years ago, Hugo came through Ledbury — he had family there, distant family. He was charming and I was — I was. Well. I was a fool. It lasted four months. He ended it, very gracefully, the way he would. And then six months ago, I heard he'd taken a cottage in Mosswick. Our Mosswick." She paused. "I told myself it was coincidence. I know it wasn't."
"No," said Miss Marple gently. "I don't think it was."
"He wanted money." The words were flat. "He came to see me privately — at the farm, in the first week after he arrived. George was in the fields. He said he had certain letters of mine, and that while he had no intention of embarrassing me, he thought I might appreciate the letters being returned and perhaps it would be worth a modest arrangement between old friends."
"Blackmail," Miss Marple said.
"Yes. Elegantly done, but yes." Nora turned to look at the water. "I told him I needed time to consider. I did not tell George. George would — George would have done something that couldn't be undone."
"Did you consider it? The arrangement?"
"I considered a great many things," Nora said, and her voice had a quality that Miss Marple listened to carefully. "I considered what I could afford, which wasn't much, because the farm's money is George's money. I considered what he would do if I refused. And then I considered other things, which I am not prepared to share with you, Miss Marple, because I am not entirely certain you are merely an elderly lady taking a walk."
Miss Marple met her gaze steadily. "You are very perceptive, Mrs. Carver."
"I grew up perceptive. My father was — never mind." She turned back toward the village path. "What I will tell you is this. George knew something was wrong. I don't know how much he knew. George doesn't ask questions directly; he watches and he waits and he draws his own conclusions. Whether those conclusions were the right ones, I honestly cannot tell you."
"Did your daughter know?"
Nora's step faltered fractionally. "Patricia is twenty-two. She's a clever girl. She sees more than either of us would like."
They walked back toward the village in a silence that was not comfortable but was honest, which is sometimes better.
At the point where the meadow path joined the village lane, Nora stopped.
"Miss Marple," she said. "Whatever you are doing — be careful. There are people in this village who are very frightened right now. Frightened people are not always rational."
"No," Miss Marple agreed. "They seldom are."

Chapter Eight: The Publican's Particular Silence
The Crossed Keys on a Saturday evening was the social centre of Mosswick in a way that Mrs. Brent-Howell's teas, for all their excellence, could not entirely replicate, because the tea was four times a year and the pub was every evening. Miss Marple did not enter it — that was not the point — but she contrived to be in the post office on Saturday morning when Bernard Croft came in to collect a parcel, and she managed an encounter of some length.
He was a careful man. She had noticed that at the tea party and she noticed it again now: the sociable surface and the careful interior, like a well-maintained building whose significant structural work is happening behind the plaster.
"Terrible business," he said, in the obligatory way. "Awful thing to happen at Dorothy's tea."
"Had you known Mr. Whitfield long?" Miss Marple asked.
"Since August. He used to come in to the Keys of an evening. Sociable type. Good company when he wanted to be."
"And when he didn't want to be?"
Croft looked at her. "He was a man who enjoyed getting people talking," he said carefully. "I serve drinks for a living. You learn to notice when a man is listening in a particular way."
"What particular way?"
"Professionally," Croft said. Then he lifted the parcel and nodded pleasantly and left.
Miss Marple watched him go and thought about the word professionally.
Hugo Whitfield had, as far as she had been able to establish, come to Mosswick with one known purpose and one potential companion in that purpose. The known purpose was Nora Carver: the letters, the blackmail, the elegant arrangement. But blackmail rarely traveled alone. A man who had found one useful secret in Mosswick might have been listening — professionally — for others.
The question was what Bernard Croft of The Crossed Keys might have said over the course of an evening's conversation that would be worth listening to professionally.
She raised this question with Dr. Pellew when he came to check on her entirely fictional bronchitis on Monday morning.
"Bernard Croft," Pellew said, and replaced his stethoscope. "Your lungs are, as I suspected, perfectly healthy."
"I never doubted it. What do you know about Bernard Croft?"
Pellew sat down. He had, Miss Marple thought, made a decision somewhere in the past few days about the nature of her inquiry and had resolved to assist it.
"Bernard Croft bought The Crossed Keys eight years ago," he said. "Came from the Midlands. He paid cash for it, which was noted at the time, because the Keys was not cheap even then."
"Cash from what source?"
"That's the question, isn't it. He was understood to have had a previous business, which he'd sold. But Bernard doesn't talk about his past in any detail. He's been a perfectly respectable publican here for eight years. Whatever happened before, nothing from before has followed him."
"Until possibly six weeks ago," Miss Marple said.
Pellew looked at her. "You think Whitfield was blackmailing Croft as well."
"I think it is possible. A man with money of unclear origin, a past he doesn't discuss, and a new arrival who was professionally listening in his pub — the arithmetic is suggestive, at least." She paused. "Did you notice anything about Bernard's behaviour at the tea? After Mr. Whitfield died?"
Pellew thought carefully. "He was — he was relieved," he said slowly. "I thought at the time it was simply that he'd found the situation less distressing than some, which is a temperamental thing. But looking back—"
"Yes," said Miss Marple. "Looking back, it could read rather differently."

Chapter Nine: Patricia
Patricia Carver came to Wisteria Lodge on Tuesday morning, which surprised Miss Marple, who had not expected to be sought out. She was shown in by Mrs. Brent-Howell and arrived in the sitting room with the direct, slightly awkward movement of a young woman who has rehearsed a conversation and is already not following the script.
She was twenty-two and had, as Ruth Addison had said, her mother's colouring: dark hair, pale skin, grey eyes. She had her father's posture — very straight — and she had something neither of them had, which was the particular quality of someone who has made a decision and is still not entirely sure it was right.
"Miss Marple," she said. "I need to talk to someone who isn't going to do something stupid."
"I rarely do anything stupid," Miss Marple said. "Do sit down."
Patricia sat. She had her hands clasped together on her knees in the way of someone preventing them from doing something more agitated.
"The night of the tea," she said. "I was in the hallway. Not the sitting room — the hallway. I'd gone to find a handkerchief, my bag was in the hallway. I was there when Hugo Whitfield came out of the sitting room and went toward the back of the house."
Miss Marple waited.
"He wasn't alone," Patricia said. "Not entirely. I mean — I didn't see who was with him. But I heard someone behind him, following him. Through the hallway door, maybe thirty seconds after he went through."
"Could you tell anything about the person from the sound?"
"The footstep was lighter than his. A woman's step, possibly. Or a slight man." She paused. "And then about four minutes later I heard the — the noise. The fall. And then Mrs. Brent-Howell."
"Did you see anyone come back through the hallway door?"
"I'd gone back into the sitting room by then. I was shaken — the noise. I thought it was an accident. And then afterward, when I thought about the footstep—"
"Why didn't you tell Inspector Harbridge?"
Patricia's hands tightened. "Because I don't know who it was. And if I said there was someone in that passage and I didn't know who, then it could have been anyone. It could have been my father."
She said the last four words with the controlled calm of someone who has said them to themselves many times.
Miss Marple was quiet for a moment.
"Your father went to find Mrs. Brent-Howell before the — the noise," she said. "I saw him leave the room. He was in the kitchen with Mrs. Brent-Howell when she found the body. She went through the hallway door and he followed her."
Patricia looked at her. "You're sure."
"I was observing the room, as you noticed. Your father was one of the people I observed. He left the sitting room approximately six minutes before Mrs. Brent-Howell's scream, walking toward the kitchen, which is accessible from the side passage off the dining room, not through the back hallway. He was in my sight until he left the room, and in Mrs. Brent-Howell's sight from the kitchen."
The relief on Patricia's face was complicated by the fact that it raised the next obvious question, which she reached almost immediately.
"Then who was it?" she said.
"That," said Miss Marple, "is what I have been working toward." She looked at her knitting and then at Patricia. "Your mother. When you came in from the hallway, was she in the sitting room?"
Patricia was very still.
"I think," she said carefully, "that she was there when I got back. But I couldn't say exactly when she returned. I was upset by the sound."
"Of course," Miss Marple said. "That's quite natural."
She did not say anything further, and Patricia, after another moment, nodded and took her leave, and Miss Marple sat with her knitting and the weight of what she now believed she knew.

Chapter Ten: A Wire and a Motive
Miss Marple sent a note to Dr. Pellew on Tuesday afternoon asking him to call, and he came at four o'clock and sat across the tea table from her with the attentiveness of a man who has understood that the conversation is likely to be important.
She laid it out for him methodically, in the order that she had assembled it, because the order mattered.
Hugo Whitfield had come to Mosswick not for the air or the scenery or the affordable rent, but because Nora Carver was here and the letters were an available asset. This much was established. But Whitfield was a professional opportunist, and an opportunist in residence does not limit his operations. He had spent six weeks in Mosswick, listening in Bernard Croft's public house, asking questions in Ruth Addison's library, and using the information gathered to expand his portfolio.
"He had at least two subjects in Mosswick," Miss Marple said. "Nora Carver and Bernard Croft. Whether there were others we cannot know. But two is sufficient to establish the pattern."
"Who killed him?" Pellew asked quietly.
"Someone arranged for him to go to the back passage at seven o'clock on Thursday evening. This was done by advance communication — a note, almost certainly, passed at the tea or delivered earlier in the day. At the bottom of the back staircase, a wire or thin cord had been fixed across the lowest uncarpeted step, anchored at two points through the wood. When Mr. Whitfield descended the stairs — and I believe he was told to come down the back stairs specifically — he caught it at the ankle and fell forward onto the flagstone floor. The head injury that killed him was caused by the fall."
"And the wire was removed afterward."
"Very quickly. The killer would have been in the passage, waiting. It would have taken seconds. In the commotion after Mrs. Brent-Howell's scream, there was ample opportunity. The wire is presumably gone entirely now."
"The marks in the carpet."
"Yes. Whoever rigged the wire needed to test it. They came to the house before the tea — the house was not locked during the day; I have confirmed this with Mrs. Brent-Howell. They attached the wire to the step using the carpet strip across the lowest step as an anchor point, tested the tension, and then removed the test anchor from the carpet, leaving those small marks. On the evening, the wire was fixed directly through holes made in the exposed wood of the step — small holes that will be there still, if Harbridge's team knows to look for them."
Pellew let out a long breath. "Who?" he said again.
"The footstep Patricia Carver heard was lighter than Whitfield's. A woman or a slight man." Miss Marple set down her knitting. "I have been thinking about Vera Dodd."
"The companion."
"Miss Dodd has been Cecily Whitfield's very great friend, as Mrs. Whitfield told me herself, for three years. During those three years, she has watched Cecily remain in a marriage that had, in practical terms, ended. She has watched Hugo Whitfield manage his wife with the same ease with which he managed everyone else. And she has watched him — because I think this is the correct chronology — move them all to Mosswick specifically to pursue an extortion scheme against a woman that Vera knew would be distressed and potentially endangered by it."
"Endangered because of George Carver."
"George Carver is not a subtle man, as everyone has told me. If he had discovered the full nature of Whitfield's arrangement with his wife, the consequences could have been serious. Vera Dodd is a clever woman. She would have understood that the risk extended beyond Nora Carver's marriage."
"But how did she know about the Carver connection?"
"She is Cecily Whitfield's companion and secretary. It is entirely possible that she manages Whitfield's correspondence and had read his — calculations. Or she heard something. Or she simply knew him well enough to understand what he was doing when he suggested coming to Mosswick."
Pellew was quiet for a moment. "And the wrist," he said slowly. "She came to me with a sprained wrist in August."
"Caught on a garden gate, she said."
"Or caught on a wire under tension." He looked at her steadily. "You're saying she came prepared. She came to Mosswick already planning this."
"Or she came to Mosswick and developed the plan once she had surveyed the ground. She is methodical. She had two months. She would have walked through Wisteria Lodge — the Brent-Howell teas are known; Mrs. Brent-Howell would have been hospitable to inquiring visitors — and she would have identified the back staircase and the hazard of the uncarpeted steps as an opportunity. A prepared opportunity, waiting for a suitable occasion."
"The tea," Pellew said. "The tea was the occasion."
"A gathering of forty minutes, all the relevant suspects present under one roof, confusion built in by numbers and noise and the consumption of sherry. The perfect setting." Miss Marple picked up her knitting. "She left the sitting room and was in the passage first. She fixed the wire. She sent — or had already sent — the note to Whitfield asking him to come to the back passage for a private conversation. He went. He descended the stairs. She removed the wire in the few seconds after the fall and before anyone came, and she was back in the sitting room or the hallway before anyone had gathered their wits."
"And the composure afterward."
"Vera Dodd is a very composed woman. Cecily Whitfield is also composed, which has its own explanation. But Vera's composure is of a specific kind: it is the composure of a person who has done what they intended and has accepted the consequence."
Pellew stood up and went to the window and stood there for a moment.
"I need to ring Harbridge," he said.
"Yes," said Miss Marple. "I think you do. And I think you should tell him about the holes in the lowest uncarpeted step, which will still be there."

Chapter Eleven: The Inspector Returns
Inspector Harbridge came to Wisteria Lodge on Wednesday morning with a sergeant and an expression that had relocated from its earlier position of comfortable certainty to something more complicated. He had found, on his re-examination of the back staircase, two small holes in the wood of the lowest step, consistent with something having been inserted and removed, and he had spoken with the county forensic examiner, who confirmed that the nature of Hugo Whitfield's head injury was consistent with a forward fall of significant momentum rather than the sideways stumble that would typically characterize an unassisted miss of a step.
He sat across from Miss Marple in Mrs. Brent-Howell's sitting room with his notebook and listened to what she told him with the careful attention of a man who is revising his understanding of a situation without allowing any visible revisions to his expression.
When she had finished, he asked several questions, all practical and sensible, and then he was quiet for a moment.
"The wire," he said. "We won't find it. She'd have gotten rid of it within the hour."
"Almost certainly."
"The holes in the step are suggestive but not conclusive. A solicitor would turn them into anything."
"Yes."
"And a woman leaving a room and returning to it, in a party of fifteen people, is not going to be verified by anyone, because no one was watching."
"No. That is, in fact, why the plan was clever." Miss Marple paused. "However, there is the question of the note. If Whitfield was directed to that passage, there was a note or there was a verbal message. If it was verbal, it was likely delivered at the tea, which means someone may have overheard it. But if it was a note—"
"We'd need to find it."
"Mr. Whitfield's body was found face down. His jacket pockets were not searched at the scene, to my knowledge."
Harbridge looked at his sergeant, who looked at his notebook.
"A handkerchief and twelve shillings," the sergeant said. "No note."
"He might have discarded it," Miss Marple said. "Or — a man who receives a note telling him to meet someone in a private passage would perhaps read it and tuck it into his waistband or his breast pocket rather than his jacket pocket. The examination of the body at the hospital—"
"I'll check," Harbridge said, and the sergeant made a note.
"There is one other thing," Miss Marple said. "The sprained wrist. Dr. Pellew can tell you about it. The manner of the injury — if it was, in fact, a wire under tension that caught her — would have left a particular mark. Not a bruise consistent with a garden gate but a linear cut or abrasion. Dr. Pellew will know whether what he saw matched what he was told."
Harbridge looked at her with an expression that was, she thought, not precisely admiration but was its more practical cousin.
"I have been a detective inspector for seventeen years," he said, "and I have never had a case broken by a guest at the tea party."
"You haven't had the case broken yet," Miss Marple said mildly. "I have given you the direction. The case is yours."
He almost smiled.

Chapter Twelve: The Note in the Breast Pocket
The note was found on Wednesday afternoon. It had been in the breast pocket of Hugo Whitfield's shirt, not his jacket, and the examination at the hospital had not thought to look there; but on Harbridge's instruction, a second look was taken and the folded paper, quite small, was there.
It said: Private matter — back passage off the hallway — seven o'clock — come alone — do not be seen.
It was typed, not handwritten, on a portable typewriter. Vera Dodd owned a portable typewriter, which she used for Cecily's correspondence. The typewriter was in Amber Cottage.
Inspector Harbridge went to Amber Cottage on Wednesday at five o'clock with the sergeant and the note in an evidence bag and a warrant that he had spent the afternoon obtaining from a magistrate who had expressed considerable surprise at the turn of events.
The typewriter matched. The worn 'e' key and the slightly elevated 'k' that are the distinctive signature of a particular machine's use were present in the note and present in a sample of typing produced from Vera Dodd's machine.
Vera Dodd, when confronted, was silent for a long moment. Cecily Whitfield was in the room and said nothing, but her face underwent a sequence of changes that no one who saw it later forgot.
Then Vera said: "He was going to destroy her. He would have taken what he wanted from Nora Carver and used it to destroy George Carver's temper and let the wreckage fall where it would. He would have kept coming back for more money from more people. He had been doing it for years. I read his notes. I know who he had hurt before. Cecily spent sixteen years with a man who was fundamentally—" She stopped.
"Miss Dodd," Harbridge said.
"I am not sorry," she said. "I wish I could be. I am not."
She accompanied the inspector with composure, and Cecily Whitfield stood in the sitting room of Amber Cottage and looked at the half-finished canvas on the easel and said nothing at all for a very long time.

Chapter Thirteen: In the Garden After Rain
The news moved through Mosswick with the speed that such news always travels, which is very fast, and by Thursday morning everyone had an opinion. Most of the opinions were complicated, which was appropriate, because the situation was complicated.
George Carver drove through the village without stopping, which those who watched said was unusual. His wife was not seen on the river path that Saturday. Patricia was seen in Ruth Addison's lending library returning three novels and borrowing three more, which was perhaps significant and perhaps not.
Bernard Croft opened The Crossed Keys at six as always and was, by most accounts, much as he always was, which might in fact have been the most information it was possible to convey.
Dr. Pellew sat in Millicent's with Miss Marple on Thursday morning — a week, almost to the hour, since the tea — and ordered coffee that he did not drink and said what he had been saying to himself for three days.
"She did it for love," he said.
"She did it for love," Miss Marple agreed, without inflection.
"And for justice, in her way."
"Yes. Those two things together are the most dangerous combination in human nature. Not greed, not anger — those are simpler. But love combined with a sincere conviction of justice is practically irresistible."
"And yet."
"And yet," Miss Marple said. "Hugo Whitfield was also a person. A very unpleasant one, who had done significant harm to a number of people and would have done more. But the wire on the step and the note in the pocket are still murder, whatever the reasons."
Pellew nodded.
"I keep thinking," he said, "about all the ways it might have been different. If Nora Carver had told someone. If Whitfield had been — what he wasn't."
"That is the particular cruelty of this kind of situation," Miss Marple said. "It needed only one person to behave differently and the whole chain of consequences falls apart. But people very rarely behave differently from their nature. That is what I have found, in rather a long life of paying attention."
She looked out at the high street, which was carrying on its usual Thursday morning activities with the imperturbable continuity of a village that has seen more than it lets on.
"George Carver didn't do it," Pellew said. "I had been afraid he had."
"He didn't know enough. And he is an obvious kind of man who would have done an obvious kind of thing. Vera Dodd is not an obvious woman."
"No." He paused. "What will happen to Cecily?"
"She will manage," Miss Marple said. "She has been managing for some time. The difficulty is that the thing she has been managing around is now — resolved, in the most terrible way, and she will have to determine what she actually wants, which is harder than enduring what one has."
"Do you think she knew? What Vera was planning?"
Miss Marple considered this with the care it deserved.
"I think she knew something was very wrong and chose not to look at it directly. That is a different thing from knowing. Human beings are remarkably good at not looking at what they do not wish to see." She paused. "The inspector will have considered the question and will not be able to prove it either way, which is probably appropriate."
"And us? What do we do now?"
"We finish our respective beverages," Miss Marple said, "and continue our respective lives. Which is, generally speaking, what one does."

Chapter Fourteen: September's End
Miss Marple's fortnight at Wisteria Lodge extended by three days, for practical reasons connected with the giving of statements, and then by a further two days because the weather had turned genuinely fine and the garden at Wisteria Lodge was producing late dahlias of a quality that deserved appreciation.
On the last morning, she sat in the garden with her knitting and her third cup of tea of the day and considered what she had observed.
She had observed, in the course of ten days in Mosswick-on-Wye, the following: that small communities are not simple, which is the first and last lesson of village life; that charm is an instrument, and like all instruments its moral quality depends entirely on the person wielding it; that love and loyalty are adjacent to each other and not the same, and the distinction matters enormously; and that the desire to protect someone can, when the wrong character is subjected to sufficient pressure, travel a very long distance from its origin.
She had also observed that the late dahlias were exceptionally good, and that Mrs. Brent-Howell's seed cake, which she had been given at intervals throughout the fortnight, was rather better than the lemon sponge, which was saying something.
Mr. Finch, her fellow lodger, came out with his own cup of tea and settled into the chair beside her with the comfortable ease of shared breakfasts.
"Harbridge fellow seems efficient," he said. He had heard about seventy percent of the previous week's events and reconstructed the remainder with reasonable accuracy from context.
"He is," Miss Marple agreed.
"Terrible business," Mr. Finch said. "Still. The Carver family. They'll be all right, I imagine. People like that generally are. Soil under their feet."
"Yes," said Miss Marple. "They'll be all right."
This was, she thought, probably true. George Carver would continue farming. Nora Carver would continue walking the river path on Saturday afternoons, and the weight she had been carrying for six weeks would be replaced by a different, older weight, which was the knowledge of what she had nearly caused by not telling anyone, and she would carry that too. Patricia would borrow novels from Ruth Addison and be, Miss Marple thought, rather all right eventually, because young people with good posture and borrowed detective novels generally are.
Cecily Whitfield would leave Amber Cottage when her lease permitted, and what she would do afterward was her own business, and Miss Marple wished her well in it with the simple sincerity of someone who had no power to do anything further and understood this.
Vera Dodd was in custody awaiting proceedings that would take the better part of a year and end in the way that such things ended. Miss Marple thought about her with the specific quality of compassion she reserved for people who were not straightforwardly innocent or guilty but something much more human and much harder to categorize. She had done a terrible thing out of love and a sincere, misguided conviction that she was protecting what mattered. This did not excuse it. It did not make it incomprehensible, either.
These two facts could coexist. Miss Marple had found, over a very long life, that most of the important things could.
Dr. Pellew came by at eleven to say goodbye and was his usual sensible self, which was its own kind of comfort.
"Will you come back?" he asked.
"I expect I shall," Miss Marple said. "Mosswick is rather a fine village."
"It is," he agreed, "though it seems to be having rather a year."
"Most places are, when you look closely enough." She clasped her carpetbag. "That is not, on reflection, a pessimistic observation. It is merely an accurate one. People are complicated, and where people are, complications follow. The only truly simple places are the ones where no one is paying attention."
She said goodbye to Mrs. Brent-Howell, who pressed upon her a small jar of last year's quince jelly and extracted a promise that she would write, and to Mr. Finch, who said it had been a great pleasure and meant it with the complete sincerity of a man who had had quite an interesting fortnight despite not having heard half of it.
The taxi came at twelve, and Miss Marple put her carpetbag and her smaller knitting bag in the back seat and looked at Mosswick-on-Wye one last time from the window as they pulled away.
The high street was going about its business. Mrs. Anstey was weighing something at the post office counter. Arnold Pruett was rearranging a display in Millicent's window. A dog was crossing the road with the purposeful authority of a dog that has business to attend to. St. Cuthbert's Norman tower stood at the east end of the village in its unperturbed way, as it had stood for eight hundred years through very much worse.
Miss Marple looked at it and thought about permanence, and about the comparative brevity of human schemes, and about how remarkable it was that people kept attempting those schemes anyway, generation after generation, in the firm conviction that this time they would work out.
She found it, on balance, rather more touching than alarming.
Then the taxi turned onto the Hereford road, and the village fell away behind her, and she picked up her knitting, and the blue wool moved steadily through her fingers all the way home.

The End
