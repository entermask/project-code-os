In the Changtang plateau of western Tibet, in the late eleventh century, at an elevation where the wind in winter came without obstruction from the northwest and the temperature at night fell to the kind of cold that is not merely uncomfortable but structurally dangerous to anything warm-blooded, there was a nomadic family whose winter grazing route brought them each year to a particular valley sheltered on three sides by limestone ridges.

The valley was not comfortable in any ordinary sense. But it was survivable. The ridges broke enough of the northwest wind to keep the temperature marginally less lethal than the open plateau. There was dried scrub grass enough for the animals under the snow if you knew where to look. There was a spring that did not freeze until the deepest weeks of the season because it emerged from the rock at a temperature slightly above what would cause it to stop.

A man named Sonam Rinchen, the second son of the family, had made this winter journey fourteen times. He was thirty-six years old. He knew the route, the valley, the spring, the places where the snow drifted and the places where the wind scoured the ground clear.

In the fifteenth year, something went wrong.

Not with the route. Not with the weather, which was severe but within the range of what the family had survived before. What went wrong was with Sonam Rinchen's mind.

The winter had brought with it a grief that the previous fourteen winters had not carried. His eldest son, a boy of twelve who had been healthy and present and real in the specific way that children are real to parents, had died in the autumn of a fever that the region's medicine could not address. The death had been rapid. The rapid quality of it had not made it easier. It had made it stranger. One week the boy was alive. The following week he was not.

Sonam Rinchen had done what the community around him understood: he had grieved. The community had supported the grief in the ways available to nomadic communities on the Changtang plateau in the eleventh century, which were real and present if not elaborate. He had performed the necessary rites. He had kept the necessary practices.

And then the winter migration had begun and they had moved as they always moved and the plateau had opened around them in its winter face and they had made their way to the valley as they always made their way.

The mind, however, had not moved on.

During the long days of travel, when the work of keeping the animals moving through the snow required enough physical attention to partially occupy the mind, it was manageable. In the evenings, when the family gathered in the tent and the butter lamps were lit and the tea was prepared and there were voices and warmth and the immediate practical demands of the night camp, it was manageable.

In the nights it was not manageable.

He lay on his sleeping mat in the dark of the tent and the mind did what it did. It returned to the boy. To the specific morning the fever had begun. To the days of the illness and the small decisions he had made, the ones he now turned over endlessly looking for the place where a different decision would have led to a different outcome. To the face of the boy on the last morning. To the weight of the boy in his arms before they carried him to the place of sky burial.

He turned all of it over, night after night, in the specific way that grief does when it is allowed to run without interruption in the dark hours.

By the sixth week of the winter camp, he was not sleeping. Not sleeping in the way that the body requires: he was lying still, but the mind was running continuously, and the running of the mind was beginning to cost the body in ways that the work of the camp required he could not afford.

There was a teacher. Not in the valley. A two-day journey north, in a cave hermitage on the far side of a ridge, there was a practitioner of the Kagyu tradition who had been there for years and who was known in the communities that moved through that region of the plateau as someone whose understanding of the mind's suffering was genuine rather than theoretical.

Sonam Rinchen made the journey in the worst week of the winter.

He arrived at the cave after two days of travel in conditions that the teacher, who had been watching the approach for the last hour from the cave entrance, described later as the kind of conditions that only a very desperate person or a very foolish one would choose to travel in. Sonam Rinchen was, the teacher said, obviously the former.

He was brought in. He was given tea. He was warmed. He slept for most of a day.

When he woke, the teacher was sitting near the small fire. He did not immediately ask what had brought the man from the valley in such weather. He waited.

Eventually Sonam Rinchen spoke. He described the nights. The mind running. The turning over of the same events, the same decisions, the same last morning. The way the running seemed to intensify in the dark, the way the cold of the plateau outside the tent seemed to make it worse rather than better.

The teacher was quiet for a long time after the man finished. Outside, the wind moved across the ridge above the cave with the sound of something very large moving very fast.

Then the teacher said: tell me. When your mind goes back to that morning, what does it expect to find?

Sonam Rinchen did not immediately understand the question.

The teacher said: every time you return to that morning in your mind, you go back as if something might be different this time. As if the returning itself might change what is there. But the morning is fixed. The morning is the same each time you visit it. What is the mind looking for that it keeps returning to find?

The man was quiet for a long time.

Then he said: I think it is looking for the place where I could have done something different.

Yes, said the teacher. And is that place there?

He was quiet again. Then, slowly: no.

The teacher said: so the mind is walking a road that ends in the same place every time. Not because the road is wrong. Because the destination is fixed. The boy is gone. The morning is over. The decisions are made. The road ends there every time and the mind cannot accept the ending and so it turns around and walks the road again hoping this time the ending will be different.

He looked at the man across the fire.

This is not grief, he said. Grief is real. Grief is the work of love releasing what it cannot keep. What you are doing in the nights is something different. You are walking a road that ends in the same wall again and again and striking the wall each time as if this time it might give way.

Sonam Rinchen sat with this for a long time. The wind continued outside.

Then he said: how do I stop walking the road?

The teacher said: you stop expecting the ending to change. Not because the ending does not matter. Because accepting the ending is the only way to stop striking the wall. The boy is gone. That is the ending. Every return to the road is another striking of the wall. The grief of his absence is real. The wall is real. The striking is what you can choose to put down.

He paused. Then he said, more quietly: you cannot think your way back to him. But you can think your way to rest.

Sonam Rinchen returned to his family's camp after three days.

The nights did not immediately become easy. The mind continued to return to the road, continued to walk toward the wall. But now when it returned, he recognized what it was doing. He had a name for it. And naming a thing, as the teacher understood and as Sonam Rinchen slowly understood, changes the relationship to the thing. The named thought is something you can see. The thought you cannot name is something you are inside without knowing it.

Slowly, across the remaining weeks of the winter, the nightly road became less urgent. Not because the grief diminished. Because the mind had stopped expecting the ending to change.

This is what the wandering mind does in the dark when it is in pain. It walks the same road to the same wall and strikes the wall and turns around and walks the road again. Not from stubbornness. From the specific desperation of love that cannot accept its ending.

If there is a road the mind has been walking tonight, returning again and again to the same place, arriving at the same fixed ending, the teacher's question is available to you now.

What does the mind expect to find when it returns?

If the answer is a different ending, the wall is real and the ending will not change.

The road can be set down tonight. Not the grief. Not the love that the grief is made of. Just the expecting of a different ending from a road that ends where it always ends.

Let the breath be long now on the exhale, slow and complete. Let the mind that has been walking be simply still for one breath. Just one. The road will be there in the morning if it is needed. Tonight, just one breath of not walking.

And as the wind moves across the limestone ridge above the cave hermitage on the Changtang plateau and the fire inside burns low, we travel now eastward and forward two centuries, to Japan, where an ink master received students who came with racing thoughts and discovered that what they needed was not what they had come for.
